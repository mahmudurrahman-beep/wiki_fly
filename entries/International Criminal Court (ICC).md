## Introduction
The **International Criminal Court (ICC)** is a permanent international tribunal established to prosecute individuals for the most serious crimes of concern to the international community: **genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and the crime of aggression**.

## History / Origins
- Established by the **Rome Statute**, adopted on **17 July 1998**.  
- Came into force on **1 July 2002**, when the required number of states ratified the treaty.  
- Headquarters: **The Hague, Netherlands**.  
- First permanent court of its kind, distinct from temporary tribunals like those for Rwanda and the former Yugoslavia.  

## Key Features
- **Jurisdiction**:  
  - Crimes committed on the territory of member states.  
  - Crimes committed by nationals of member states.  
  - Crimes referred by the **UN Security Council**.  
- **Membership**: 124 countries are parties to the Rome Statute (as of 2025).  
- **Structure**:  
  - Presidency.  
  - Judicial Divisions (Pre‚ÄëTrial, Trial, Appeals).  
  - Office of the Prosecutor.  
  - Registry (administrative support).  
- **Notable Cases**:  
  - Trials of leaders and military officials accused of war crimes and crimes against humanity.  
  - Investigations in Africa, the Middle East, Eastern Europe, and beyond.  

## Legacy / Impact
- Represents a milestone in international justice, aiming to end impunity for grave crimes.  
- Criticized by some states for perceived bias or political influence.  
- Several major powers (e.g., USA, China, Russia, India) are **not members**, limiting its reach.  
- Despite challenges, the ICC remains central to global efforts for accountability and human rights.
