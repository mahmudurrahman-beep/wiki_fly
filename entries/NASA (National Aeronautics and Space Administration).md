## Definition
**NASA** is the United States government agency responsible for the nation’s civilian space program and for aeronautics and aerospace research. It leads space exploration, scientific discovery, and technological innovation.

## Origins
- Established: **July 29, 1958**, by the National Aeronautics and Space Act.  
- Replaced the earlier **NACA (National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics)**.  
- Created in response to the Soviet Union’s launch of **Sputnik (1957)**.  

## Headquarters & Structure
- Headquarters: **Washington, D.C.**  
- Major centers: Kennedy Space Center (Florida), Johnson Space Center (Texas), Jet Propulsion Laboratory (California), Goddard Space Flight Center (Maryland), and others.  
- Administrator: Appointed by the U.S. President.  

## Key Achievements
- **1969**: Apollo 11 – first humans on the Moon (Neil Armstrong, Buzz Aldrin).  
- **1970s**: Skylab space station and Apollo‑Soyuz Test Project.  
- **1981–2011**: Space Shuttle program.  
- **1998–present**: Partnership in the **International Space Station (ISS)**.  
- **2012**: Curiosity rover lands on Mars.  
- **2021**: James Webb Space Telescope launched.  
- **2024–2030s**: Artemis program – returning humans to the Moon, preparing for Mars.  

## Current Focus
- **Artemis Program** – human exploration of the Moon and beyond.  
- **Mars Missions** – rovers like Perseverance and plans for human missions.  
- **Earth Science** – climate monitoring, satellites for weather and environment.  
- **Astrophysics** – telescopes studying the universe (Hubble, JWST).  
- **Commercial Partnerships** – working with SpaceX, Blue Origin, and others.  

## Legacy
- NASA has become a symbol of human curiosity and exploration.  
- Its missions have advanced science, technology, and international cooperation.  
- Continues to inspire generations with the vision of exploring beyond Earth.
