## Definition
**Jainism** (Jain Dharma) is an ancient Indian religion that teaches a path toward spiritual purity and liberation through strict non‑violence and ascetic discipline. Followers are known as **Jains**.

## Core Principles
- **Ahimsa (Non‑violence)** – Respect for all living beings.  
- **Anekantavada (Non‑absolutism)** – Reality is many‑sided; truth can be seen from multiple perspectives.  
- **Aparigraha (Non‑possession)** – Detachment from material and emotional possessions.  

## Tirthankaras
- Jainism is guided by **24 Tirthankaras** (spiritual teachers).  
- The first was **Rishabhanatha (Adinatha)**.  
- The 24th and last was **Mahavira (599–527 BCE)**, who revitalized Jainism.  

## Practices
- **Asceticism** – Renunciation of worldly pleasures.  
- **Meditation & Fasting** – Spiritual purification.  
- **Vegetarianism** – Strict diet avoiding harm to living beings.  
- **Rituals & Festivals** – Paryushana, Mahavir Jayanti.  

## Sects
- **Digambara ("Sky‑clad")** – Monks renounce all possessions, including clothing.  
- **Svetambara ("White‑clad")** – Monks wear simple white robes.  

## Literature
- Sacred texts include the **Agamas** and commentaries.  
- Written in Prakrit, Sanskrit, and later vernacular languages.  

## Legacy
- Jainism has influenced Indian philosophy, art, and ethics.  
- Today, Jains are a small but influential community, especially in India.  
- Jain principles of non‑violence inspired figures like **Mahatma Gandhi**.  

---

*Jainism remains a living tradition, blending ascetic ideals with practical ethics, and continues to inspire global movements for peace and non‑violence.*
