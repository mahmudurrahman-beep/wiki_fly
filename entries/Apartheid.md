# Apartheid in South Africa

## Origins
- The word **“apartheid”** means *“apartness”* in Afrikaans.  
- Though racial segregation existed earlier, it became official policy when the **National Party** came to power in **1948**.  
- Designed to maintain white minority dominance over the Black majority.  

## Key Features
- **Population Registration Act (1950):** Classified citizens by race.  
- **Group Areas Act (1950):** Forced relocation of nonwhites to separate areas.  
- **Pass Laws:** Required Black South Africans to carry identification to travel.  
- **Separate facilities:** Schools, hospitals, beaches, buses, and even benches were segregated.  
- **Political exclusion:** Black South Africans denied voting rights and representation.  

## Resistance
- **African National Congress (ANC)** and other groups organized protests and strikes.  
- **Sharpeville Massacre (1960):** Police killed 69 protesters, sparking global outrage.  
- **Soweto Uprising (1976):** Student protests against Afrikaans in schools, met with violence.  
- Leaders like **Nelson Mandela** and **Oliver Tambo** became symbols of resistance.  

## International Pressure
- Global sanctions, boycotts, and condemnation increased in the 1980s.  
- The UN declared apartheid a crime against humanity.  
- Sports and cultural isolation further pressured the regime.  

## End of Apartheid
- **1990:** Nelson Mandela released from prison after 27 years.  
- **1994:** First democratic elections held; Mandela became South Africa’s first Black president.  
- Apartheid laws were repealed, and a new constitution guaranteed equal rights.  

## Legacy
- Left deep social and economic inequalities that persist today.  
- Truth and Reconciliation Commission (1996) investigated human rights abuses.  
- Symbolizes both the brutality of racial oppression and the triumph of democracy.
