## Introduction
**Economic liberalism** is a political and economic philosophy that advocates for an economy driven by **individual choice and market forces**, rather than heavy government regulation. It is closely associated with **classical economics** and the ideas of **Adam Smith**, who argued that the ‚Äúinvisible hand‚Äù of the market leads to efficient outcomes.

## History / Origins
- Emerged in the **18th century Enlightenment**, particularly in Britain and France.  
- Adam Smith‚Äôs *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) laid the foundation for liberal economic thought.  
- Developed as a reaction against **mercantilism**, which emphasized state control and accumulation of bullion.  
- Spread globally in the 19th century, influencing industrial capitalism and international trade.  

## Key Features
- **Free Markets**: Prices determined by supply and demand.  
- **Private Property**: Individuals and businesses own resources and capital.  
- **Limited Government**: State‚Äôs role restricted to protecting property rights, enforcing contracts, and maintaining security.  
- **Free Trade**: Advocates removal of tariffs and barriers to international commerce.  
- **Competition**: Encourages innovation, efficiency, and consumer choice.  

## Legacy / Impact
- Became the foundation of **capitalism** and modern global trade.  
- Influenced policies of deregulation, privatization, and tax reduction in the 20th century.  
- Criticized for contributing to inequality and financial instability, leading to debates about balancing markets with social protections.
