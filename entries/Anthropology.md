## Definition
**Anthropology** is the scientific study of humans, human behavior, and societies in the past and present. It explores cultural, biological, linguistic, and archaeological aspects of humanity.

## Branches of Anthropology
1. **Cultural Anthropology**  
   - Studies cultural practices, beliefs, and social structures.  
   - Uses ethnography (fieldwork and participant observation).  

2. **Biological (Physical) Anthropology**  
   - Examines human evolution, genetics, and biological diversity.  
   - Studies fossils, primates, and adaptation.  

3. **Archaeology**  
   - Investigates past human societies through material remains.  
   - Uses excavation and analysis of artifacts, structures, and landscapes.  

4. **Linguistic Anthropology**  
   - Studies language and its role in culture and society.  
   - Explores how language shapes identity, communication, and worldview.  

## Methods
- **Fieldwork** – Living among communities to observe and record practices.  
- **Excavation** – Recovering artifacts and fossils.  
- **Comparative Analysis** – Studying similarities and differences across cultures.  
- **Interdisciplinary Research** – Combining insights from history, sociology, biology, and linguistics.  

## Importance
- Helps understand human diversity and commonality.  
- Provides insights into cultural traditions, social change, and globalization.  
- Informs debates on race, identity, and human rights.  
- Preserves knowledge of past civilizations.  

## Famous Anthropologists
- **Franz Boas** – Father of American anthropology.  
- **Margaret Mead** – Known for studies of adolescence and culture.  
- **Claude Lévi‑Strauss** – Developed structuralist approaches to culture.  
- **Bronisław Malinowski** – Pioneer of ethnographic fieldwork.
